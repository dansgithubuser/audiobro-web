Intro

Audiobro is an amateur album made with Dan's Live Audio Lab (dlal). Started in September 2017 and released in August 2024, it served as a "focus" to the ever-expansive exploration in dlal. The album was created with the help of my audio bro. We had an established pattern of hanging out and jamming something up. I suggested to start recording our jam sessions so I could take each recording back and turn it into a track. Originally the goal was 3 jam sessions and 3 tracks, but the 4th track struck me and stuck with me, and the 5th is based off a performance we did in 2009.

Release Goals

I'm not going to bother putting together a license. I have no idea what that means and I'm not (presently) interested to find out. Instead I'd like to describe what I want to get out of releasing this album. Let's do a list:
  • If you're a coder and audio enthusiast, and you're interested in what you heard, consider taking a look at dlal.
  • If you know me personally, I'd like you to enjoy these tracks and if you feel like it, talk to me about them.
  • If you're a creator, feel free to use the tracks, but please don't do silly thinks like claim them as your own, and try to find a place to link this page. Email me if you want some modified version of a track.
  • I don't want money, because it introduces more logistics around not-creating than I'm willing to accept. If something viral happens, I reserve the right to revise this.

Details by Track (spoilers?)

Track 1

After explaining my idea and having the first jam session (at his place and on his setup), I sent an email with the subject "yo send the audio bro". This is the only thing that ever popped out to me as an appropriate album title.

Once the minor loop was established, my audio bro tried to fit a major line in (the bells in the final track). This is the kind of thing we'd try to do: make a pattern, then carefully throw a wrench in it.

Burgers

The second jam session happened at my place, and we had some burgers on a cheap charcoal barbecue beforehand. Afterward we repeated and recorded some of our conversation. While barbecuing, I came up with the following burger recipe:

  • Take the stem off a portabello mushroom, and cook it.
  • Turn the mushroom upside down and put brie cheese in. This allows the brie to melt without escaping the mushroom.
  • Take a plain burger (bun, patty), add the mushroom and brie, and add pickles (do I want a pickle on my burger?).
I was so pleased with the result, I said to my bro, "People, they think they know burgers, but they haven't been here."

I think it had been a while since I used the barbecue and so I forgot it had legs. Next time we'll find its legs.

The Legend of Bass Man

The jam session behind this track really demonstrates the nature of audio bros and careful wrench-throwing. Halfway into a tour of the instruments at my place, my audio bro starts reciting (improvising) The Legend of Bass Man. When tracking, I felt the only true artistic implementation was to make a robotic singing voice to recite The Legend. It's also a topic I was previously interested in. By robotic I mean the artist should be able to write down notes and (phonetic) lyrics and get a singing voice out of it, with complete control. It would also be great to figure out how to do it live so it could be performed with. I knew this would be hard, but it was harder (and therefore also took longer) than I thought it would. Nonetheless, the learnings (remember, the whole point of the album is to focus audio explorations in dlal) kept paying off.

I avoided deep learning speech synthesis for a while, because I knew with deep speech, I couldn't have control over pitch and timing to make it sing. Eventually though, two things happened. First, I became somewhat confident the only barrier between me and intelligible speech was details in how phonetics work. For example, the aspiration (h sound) that follows the p in "pick". My goal was never to deal with how phonetics work in any particular language, only to synthesize a good phonetic description into a singing robot. Second, I learned enough to take an intelligible utterance, take phonetic information out of it, and arbitrarily pick how that phonetic information is timed and pitched. In the end, this is what I went with to get the track done. This method will not work in a live setting, because the deep speech generation is slow, and the timing is manual. Some day I'd like to figure out how to get a robot voice working in a live setting.

Track 4

The concept for this track that struck me and stuck with me is this: take the bass riff from track 1 and turn it into an orchestral tragedy. Then, take the riff out. At the end, put it back in as a punchline. I'm a big fan of cerebral musical jokes like this, and I think they're present in the whole album. Another way I think of it: any short segment of the album should sound like it is taking itself seriously, but beyond that there should be an eclectic wackiness.

Bassindaface / Funky Funky Bass

In 2009 my audio bro and I decided to do a performance at something like a talent show. I had a bassoon and he had a bass guitar. We created the concept "Bassindaface" where we'd be trying to out-bass each other. The lowest note on a bassoon is a Bb1 (~58 Hz); the lowest note on a bass guitar is E1 (~41 Hz) and they follow from the question, "What's the lowest note you can play?". The diminished riff follows from those two notes.

Funky Funky Bass is the onomatopoeia of a different bassline I had invented previously that I offered to Bassindaface.

The performance featured the basslines in an ABA format, and us telling a bunch of bad bass puns. At the end, audio bro detuned the lowest string so the note was lower. I reinterpreted this into the 5th track in a way I suspect most people won't notice. If you have perfect pitch, I'm curious if anything seems funny.

Specific Influences

There's some specific pieces of music that I love that I used as inspiration for some passages, that I'd like to mention as a celebration of them. I hope I've done a good job reinterpreting the musical ideas so as not to be derivative.
  • Power Flower 1 from Sub-Terrania, opening chords into the track 1 midsection chords.
  • Holst's Neptune for polyrhythm arpeggios, tracks 2 and 4.
  • D/Generation intro song, which I confused with the Sim City 2000 Budget Screen. In track 2 midsection.
  • Ecco the Dolphin: The Tides of Time, Title Theme in a few places, but especially the 4th track.
  • A specific build-up from Beast Wars that I have no idea how to track down, but I (think I) remember it went up in minor 3rds. Minor 3rd chord changes are a tiny and therefore common idea, but I tried to recreate the feeling that stuck with me in the track 5 opening.
  • Moonlight Sonata, 3rd Movement, those diminished chords toward the end. In the 5th track when we get back to the Bassindaface riff.
  • Descent, Level 22, mid-section bassline fit perfectly with Bassindaface.
  • Dancing Mad from Final Fantasy 6, and In-A-Gadda-Da-Vida for the structure of the 5th track. The eclecticness of styles in these tracks really match the vibe I want.
  • Descent, Level 8. This is more of an honorable/loving mention because I can't find a place in the album to point. But the initial chord change is always kicking around in my head.
